The Battle of Hastings

14 October 1066 — East Sussex, England

Overview

The Battle of Hastings was fought on 14 October 1066 between the Norman-French army of William, the Duke of Normandy, and the English army led by King Harold II. It was the decisive Norman victory that changed the course of English history forever.

Background

When King Edward the Confessor died in January 1066 without a clear heir, three men claimed the English throne — Harold Godwinson, Harald Hardrada of Norway, and William of Normandy. Harold was crowned king the same day Edward died, which set the stage for two invasions.

The Battle

William's forces landed at Pevensey on 28 September 1066. Harold, having just defeated Hardrada in the north, marched his exhausted army south. The battle lasted most of the day. Harold was killed — according to legend, by an arrow through the eye — and English resistance collapsed.

Aftermath

William was crowned King of England on Christmas Day 1066 at Westminster Abbey. The Norman conquest transformed English language, culture, law, and society in ways still felt today.

Quick Facts

Date 14 October 1066
Location East Sussex, England
Result Norman Victory
Key Figure William of Normandy
Casualties Unknown — estimated thousands
